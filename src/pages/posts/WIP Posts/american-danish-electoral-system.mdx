Being a dork who thinks too much about too many things, I've thought a lot about politics throughout my life. Most of this time has been spent
on systems rather than policies or parties, and one common thing I've done is re-imagining countries with different electoral system, mostly
redoing US elections using other forms of governance.

Recently I had an idea to do the inverse, to apply the US electoral system to my home country of Denmark. This would mean replacing our fairly
decent proportionally representative system with a series of first-past-the-post constituencies that collectively distort the voice of the people.
I'm going to completely ignore the existence of the presidency, as that's not as straight-forward to simulate on existing data.

## The Danish electoral system

To begin with, let's quickly go over how the Danish electoral system works in reality. The country is split into 10 Storkredse ("Great Circles")
made up of 92 Opstillingskredse ("Nomination Circles"). Additionally, 
<a href="https://www.thedanishparliament.dk/-/media/sites/ft/pdf/publikationer/the-constitutional-act-of-denmark.pdf">the constitution</a> tells us
that Greenland and the Faeroe Islands each get to send 2 MPs, essentially making them 2 Storkredse with a single Opstillingskreds each. 

(Insert Picture here of the storkredse)

Each Storkreds gets a number of Kredsmandater ("Circle Mandates") proportional to their population and population density (redistributed every 5 years), 
and a number of Tillægsmandater ("Additional Mandates"). The Kredsmandater are distributed according to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Hondt_method">
D'Hondt</a> method, whereafter the difference between the national voteshare and MP-count is calculated for all parties, and the Tillægsmandater
are assigned to their parties to best equalise the two.

For more information on the current system, please consult <a href="https://www.elections.im.dk/media/15735/the-electoral-system-in-denmark.pdf">
the Danish Interior Ministry's documentation</a>.

## Applying the US electoral system

The US operates in a <s>much worse</s> very different manner. The country is split into 50 states with 435 constituencies. Each state elects 2
senators (in two different first past the post races) and each constituency elects a single representative. 

(I am deliberately ignoring the 6 year cycle of senators right now, and treating it all as one big race)

For the purposes of our system, we shall consider each Storkreds to be a state and each Opstillingskreds to be a constituency. Each Storkreds gets to
elect 2 senators, and each Opstillingskreds gets to elect 1 representative.

This approach does come withone significant downside: while mandates are reproportioned every 5 years, Opstillingskredse are not redrawn or re-distributed, 
since mandates are distributed in the entire Storkreds. This results in Opstillingskredse having between 16K and 60K people. Whilst this is in absolute terms
less than the differences in the US (where the smallest district is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhode_Island's_1st_congressional_district">RI-1</a> 
with 500K, and the largest is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delaware's_at-large_congressional_district">DE</a> with ~1M people), it's a much
bigger difference proportionally.
